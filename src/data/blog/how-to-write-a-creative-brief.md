The single biggest predictor of a successful design project is not the designer's talent. It's the quality of the brief.

A great brief gives a designer everything they need to make bold, informed creative decisions. A poor brief produces timid, generic work — or worse, an expensive cycle of revisions where nobody is quite sure what they're aiming for.

After years of receiving briefs from clients — and helping clients who have been burned by bad design relationships — here's the definitive guide to writing one that works.

## What a Creative Brief Is (And Isn't)

A creative brief is a **strategic document**, not a design specification. It should communicate context, goals, constraints, and direction — not dictate colours, fonts, or layouts.

The most common mistake clients make is writing a brief that's actually a design specification: "I want a blue logo with a mountain and the tagline 'Reach New Heights' in a serif font." This removes creative agency from the designer without providing the strategic context that would help them make better decisions than you would.

The brief should answer: *Why does this business exist, who does it serve, and what feeling should the brand evoke?* Not: *What should it look like?*

## The Seven Elements of a Strong Brief

### 1. Business Overview

A concise description of what your business does, who your customers are, and how you make money. Three to five sentences maximum. If you can't describe your business in five sentences, that's the first problem to solve.

Include:
- What you do and how
- Who your primary customer is (specific, not "everyone")
- The problem you solve for them
- How long you've been operating and key context (startup, rebrand, expansion)

### 2. The Project Objective

What are you trying to achieve with this design project, and how will you measure success?

Be specific:
- "We need a logo and brand identity for a new coffee roasting company launching in March" ✓
- "We want to rebrand to feel more premium and attract enterprise clients" ✓
- "We need a logo" ✗ (too vague — why? for what purpose? by when?)

### 3. Target Audience

Describe your customer in enough detail that a designer could picture them. Demographics are a starting point — psychographics are more useful.

Not: "Women aged 25–45."  
Better: "Women aged 28–42, professionally established, health-conscious, willing to pay a premium for quality, values authenticity over flash, shops at independent retailers rather than chains, follows wellness accounts on Instagram."

A designer who understands your customer can make visual decisions that resonate with them. A demographic age range tells them almost nothing useful.

### 4. Brand Personality and Values

If your brand were a person, how would you describe them? This is one of the most useful exercises in any branding project.

Choose 3–5 adjectives that describe your brand's character:
- *Warm, knowledgeable, approachable, precise, quietly confident*
- *Bold, irreverent, energetic, direct, fun*
- *Calm, trustworthy, premium, minimal, sophisticated*

Then identify 2–3 words that explicitly do NOT describe you — these guard rails are equally valuable. "We are not corporate. We are not trendy. We are not cheap-looking."

### 5. Competitive Landscape

Who are your main competitors? Share their websites and logos. Identify what you want to be clearly different from — and if there's anything in the competitive space that's done well, note that too.

This is not about copying. It's about understanding the visual language of your category so the designer knows what conventions to follow and which to deliberately break.

### 6. Inspiration and Direction

Share 5–10 examples of design work you admire — not necessarily in your industry. Explain *why* you're drawn to each one. "I like how this feels premium without being cold" is far more useful than "I like this logo."

Tools like Pinterest boards, Behance collections, or simple screenshot folders all work.

Be honest if something is outside your comfort zone too. "I'm drawn to this minimal direction but slightly nervous it won't communicate enough warmth" is useful information.

### 7. Practical Constraints

The logistical reality that affects design decisions:
- Budget range (gives the designer appropriate scope)
- Timeline and key milestones
- Deliverables needed (logo files only? Full brand guidelines? Packaging? Web assets?)
- Technical constraints (e.g., must work embroidered on uniforms, must work in black and white for certain applications)
- Any non-negotiables (e.g., "we must keep the colour green — it's been part of our brand for 20 years")

## What to Leave Out

**Don't include:**
- Specific colour codes or font names (unless they're non-negotiable for a reason)
- Rough sketches of logo ideas you've drawn yourself (this anchors the designer to your idea rather than the best idea)
- Design directions you're including "just to be safe" — conflicting directions produce confused output
- Instructions derived from things you personally dislike but your audience might love

Trust the brief to communicate the *why*. Trust the designer to determine the *what*.

## The Brief Review Test

Before sending your brief, read it back and ask:

- Could a stranger read this and understand exactly what your business does and who it serves?
- Does it communicate a clear creative direction without prescribing a specific design solution?
- Are the constraints realistic and clearly explained?
- Does it give enough information to make 10 different creative decisions?

If the answer to all four is yes, you've written a strong brief — and you're almost guaranteed to get better design work than you would have otherwise.

The investment of two hours writing a great brief saves ten hours of revision cycles and one very frustrated designer.
